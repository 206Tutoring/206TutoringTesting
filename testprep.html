THE MOST PAINLESS KIND OF TEST PREP

Education is no longer a means for upward social mobility; it is now almost a prerequisite. We’d like to to measure the value of our own education in friends and memories made, and the most fun I have with this job is teaching English, math, and piano with personal growth in mind. However, the college-admissions process and the job market tend to take a less holistic view of things. So test prep becomes necessary.

I can teach most tests, especially the SAT and ACT, efficiently and effectively. My lessons are a better product than my competitors offer due to lower rates, individual attention, and my exceptional test scores, far above what you'll get at Kaplan or the Princeton Review.

At any rate, though test-taking is unpleasant, challenging, and only a means to an end, these ends — an ideal college experience (or at least a college experience of one's choice), getting into a professional or graduate program, obtaining a lucrative and challenging job — are worthwhile enough that test-prep is one of the easiest and cheapest investments you can make.

I’m confident that I can offer a very good return on this investment. For a little time and money, I can teach most anyone how to master the SAT, ACT, GRE, or a handful of assorted AP Tests, enabling everyone to devote themselves to happy and successful lives during, before, and after the tests. Because of the one-on-one nature of my tutoring business, and the absence of a middleman and rigid curricula, my sessions are a better value than any test-prep agency.

MY TEST-TAKING TRACK RECORD

- My SAT scores were 800 in Math, 800 in Reading, and 770 in Writing. This is well above the standard for a Princeton Review instructor, who need only score 700 when he starts teaching, and that for a Kaplan instructor. As someone who took the SAT twice — the format changed my senior year of high school — as well as an older sibling and educator of seven years’ experience, I’m well aware of what’s changed on the SAT, and what’s stayed the same.

- My GRE scores were 800 in Verbal and 790 in Math (1590 translates to 339 using the current scoring scale.) The GRE and the SAT, in spite of their recent ‘overhauls,’ are still the old school of standardized tests that were developed prior to the emergence of the test-prep industry, and can thus be prepared for and mastered in the same efficient way.

- I got 5’s on the AP US History, Calculus AB, Calculus BC, and Statistics tests, and 4’s on the English Language and Composition and European History tests. Due to my coursework at Columbia and my 15 years of playing the piano, I also teach the Music Theory test. For the history and English exams, I am deeply familiar with the AP ‘formula’ to (pretend to) assess quality of writing, and thus can offer general skills for conquering future tests inside and outside the AP.

- Though I never took the ACT, I have ample experience teaching the ACT and am well-apprised of the differences and similarities between it and the SAT/GRE. My students have made comparable gains on both the ACT and the SAT.

TEACHING QUALIFICATIONS

- As a student and educator, I’ve taken hundreds (if not thousands) of tests, and can impart a framework for studying for any test, as well as ways to deal with the experience of preparing for and taking tests, which will translate well into future ‘high-stakes’ situations like college or job interviews.

- I’m well-versed in the ‘strategic’ aspect of test-taking. Some examples include:     
    - How to adapt to an ‘adaptive’ test
    - The game theory of guessing     
    - Management of time, emotions, and fatigue     
    - (For reading tests) ‘Reading’ what kind of multiple-choice answer is most likely right     
    - (For writing tests) ‘Reading’ the subjective graders for what they’re looking for     
    - (For math tests) Focusing on getting right the long-form problems you know you can

- As a human being, I recognize that the experience of school as stress and anxiety is largely due to classes being ‘taught to the test,’ and stress and anxiety therefore too often define the experience of those tests. The biggest difficulty on tests is not a failure to grasp the material, but a failure to remember it in time, due to some combination of nerves and fatigue and poor motivation. Test scores therefore respond even more positively to putting you in healthier and more relaxed state of mind, which is a great strength of mine as a tutor.
